The Nuclear Fuel Bottleneck

How a 40× gap between HALEU supply and demand is shaping a $200 billion rebuilding of the Western enrichment infrastructure.

Editor: RohanShilohShah@outlook.com

I

Physics First: The Fission Chain

Consider the arithmetic of the ground. Every tonne of uranium ore extracted from the Athabasca Basin in Saskatchewan or the steppes of Kazakhstan contains roughly 7 kilograms of U-235 — the isotope capable of sustaining a chain reaction — and 993 kilograms of U-238, which is chemically identical but nearly inert for power generation purposes. That 0.7% fraction of U-235 is where all commercial nuclear energy begins.

What makes U-235 special is its response to neutron bombardment. When a slow-moving neutron collides with a U-235 nucleus, the nucleus absorbs it, destabilizes, and splits — releasing, in the process, roughly 200 million electron volts of kinetic energy. For comparison: combusting a single carbon atom releases about 4 electron volts. The energy density ratio between nuclear fission and chemical combustion is approximately 50 million to one. One gram of U-235 fully fissioned matches the energy content of roughly three tonnes of coal.

Fission also releases two or three additional neutrons per event. Under the right conditions — the right geometry, the right neutron speeds, the right U-235 concentration — those neutrons trigger further fissions, which release more neutrons, which trigger more fissions. The cascade compounds into a self-sustaining chain reaction.

Core Concept
Criticality and k-effective
Physicists describe chain reaction behavior with k-effective (keff), the ratio of neutrons in each generation to neutrons in the previous one. keff = 1 is critical — steady power output. keff > 1 is supercritical — exponential energy increase. keff < 1 is subcritical — the reaction dies. Every reactor control system exists to hold keff at exactly 1.000, continuously, for years.

Managing that cascade requires three things. Fuel — uranium with enough U-235 concentration that the chain can sustain itself in the available geometry. A moderator — a material that slows the fast neutrons emitted by fission to lower "thermal" velocities, at which they are substantially more effective at triggering further U-235 fissions. And a coolant — a medium to extract heat from the core and carry it to a turbine.

In the pressurized water reactors that generate the majority of nuclear electricity in the United States, France, China, and Japan, ordinary water serves as both moderator and coolant simultaneously — a design simplification that has shaped the entire Western enrichment infrastructure. Water moderation requires a minimum U-235 concentration of roughly 3–5% to sustain criticality. That number is the origin of all the constraints we are about to walk through.

Isotope
U-235 and U-238 are chemically indistinguishable — same atomic number, same electron configuration, same bonding behavior. Only their mass differs by 1.26%. That gap is the entire physical basis of the enrichment industry.
"One gram of U-235 fully fissioned matches the energy content of roughly three tonnes of coal." The 50-million-to-one energy density ratio that makes nuclear power physically possible.
Fast vs. Thermal
Neutrons emitted by fission travel at ~20,000 km/s. "Thermal" neutrons move at ~2.2 km/s. Water molecules slow neutrons through repeated elastic collisions with hydrogen atoms — reducing velocity by a factor of roughly 10,000 across the cascade.
II

Next-Generation Designs and Their Fuel Requirements

For half a century, the 3–5% U-235 enrichment level was treated as a fixed engineering constraint — the invariant around which reactor geometry, control systems, refueling schedules, and coolant chemistry were all optimized. The new generation of reactors represents a deliberate decision to relax that constraint, and the consequences are categorical rather than incremental.

Push enrichment to between 5% and 20% U-235 — the range now designated High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, or HALEU — and the fissile density of the fuel increases by a factor of three to four over conventional fuel. This unlocks reactor geometries and coolant chemistries that are structurally inaccessible at conventional enrichment levels.

Key Term
HALEU — High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium
Uranium enriched to 5–20% U-235. The boundary at 20% is not arbitrary: above that threshold, material is classified as highly enriched uranium (HEU) under international nonproliferation law. HALEU sits at the maximum civilian-legal fissile density. Currently produced at commercial scale in only two countries: Russia and China.

With HALEU-fueled cores, reactor dimensions shrink to the point where factory fabrication and road transport become viable. Operating cycles between refuelings extend from 18 months to a decade or more. Coolant alternatives become practical — liquid sodium (boiling point 883°C), molten salt, helium gas — each enabling temperatures far above the ~320°C ceiling of pressurized water. Higher coolant temperature means higher thermodynamic efficiency at the turbine, and the ability to supply industrial process heat, not just electricity.

Design Principle
Passive Safety
Water-cooled reactors require continuous active cooling — pumps, pressure management, operator action — to prevent core overheating if the chain reaction is disrupted. Certain HALEU-fueled designs using high-boiling-point coolants achieve passive shutdown: if all active systems fail and no one intervenes, the reactor extinguishes through natural convection. This is a thermodynamic consequence of the fuel and coolant chemistry, not a regulatory designation.

TerraPower's Natrium reactor, backed by Bill Gates and under construction in Wyoming, uses liquid sodium as its primary coolant. The sodium circuit operates at approximately 500°C — nearly 200°C above the maximum for pressurized water — enabling a materially more efficient steam cycle. Sodium cooling at these parameters requires HALEU-enriched fuel.

X-energy's Xe-100, backed by Amazon, packs HALEU-enriched uranium into golf-ball-sized graphite-matrix pebbles that circulate through the core. The pebble-bed geometry makes uncontrolled power excursion physically impossible — but only because each pebble carries fuel dense enough for the geometry to sustain criticality in the first place.

Oklo's Aurora microreactor is designed to operate for a decade without refueling or on-site staff — a nuclear battery in industrial building form. The operating lifetime and footprint only close at HALEU enrichment levels.

The performance case for next-generation designs is genuine: smaller, safer, longer-lived, more thermally efficient, and far faster to deploy than the existing fleet. Every advantage on that list is contingent on fuel that, outside Russia and China, does not currently exist at commercial scale in the West.

Size Comparison
A conventional pressurized water reactor: ~1,000 MW, built on-site over 10–15 years, typically $8–15B total cost. An SMR: 50–300 MW, factory-built, delivered to site. The economic premise is that replication economies replace scale economies as the dominant cost driver.
"Every advantage of next-generation designs is contingent on fuel that, outside Russia and China, does not currently exist at commercial scale in the West."
Sodium History
Liquid sodium as a reactor coolant was first demonstrated at EBR-I, Idaho, in 1951 — the first reactor to generate usable electricity. The technology is 70 years old. The supply chain for the fuel it requires is not.
III

Centrifuges, Cascades, and the Cost of Separation

The technical barrier to enrichment begins with a single physical fact: U-235 and U-238 are chemically indistinguishable. No chemical process can separate them. The only exploitable difference is mass — U-238 is approximately 1.26% heavier than U-235.

The workhorse of modern enrichment is the gas centrifuge. Uranium ore concentrate is converted into uranium hexafluoride — UF₆, a compound that gasifies at mild temperatures — and fed into a centrifuge rotor spinning at 400 to 500 meters per second. At those velocities, UF₆ molecules carrying the heavier U-238 atoms drift fractionally toward the outer wall; molecules carrying U-235 concentrate toward the center. The separation per pass is measured in fractions of a percent.

To achieve useful enrichment levels, this marginal effect must be compounded across thousands of machines connected in series — a cascade. Slightly enriched product from one centrifuge feeds the next stage; depleted "tails" exit from the other end. After enough stages, the output stream reaches the target U-235 concentration.

Unit of Measure
The Separative Work Unit (SWU)
Pronounced "swoo." The SWU captures the total thermodynamic work — energy, capital, and time — that a centrifuge cascade must expend to achieve a given separation of a given mass of uranium. It is the standard commodity unit of the enrichment market. The U.S. nuclear fleet consumes ~15 million SWUs annually. Russia produces ~28 million SWUs of capacity. All of North America combined: ~4.3 million. The 6.5× capacity gap defines the vulnerability at the center of Western nuclear fuel supply.

Two features of enrichment matter for investment analysis. First, the technology is genuinely difficult to replicate. Centrifuge rotors must maintain dimensional integrity at supersonic velocities for years without maintenance; the bearing systems and cascade balancing represent engineering problems that took decades to solve. Fewer than ten nations have demonstrated commercial-scale enrichment capability, and the specifications remain classified in most of them.

Second, the SWU market is structurally lopsided in a way that has no precedent in other commodity markets. The United States consumes 15 million SWUs per year. Russia produces 28 million SWUs of capacity — nearly twice U.S. annual demand, from a single country. The mismatch between Western consumption and Western production capacity is not a transient imbalance. It is the product of forty years of policy choices that treated Russian enrichment as a feature rather than a risk.

UF₆
Uranium hexafluoride — the process gas of the global enrichment industry. A yellow crystalline solid at room temperature; it sublimes directly to gas when gently warmed. Highly corrosive and toxic. Every gram of enriched uranium on earth passed through this form.
Centrifuge Physics
Modern rotor tips spin at Mach 1.3+, continuously, for years. The magnetic bearing and vacuum systems that make this possible are themselves advanced engineering problems. A single cascade halt costs weeks of lost production and risks rotor damage.
"Russia: 28M SWU capacity. All of North America: 4.3M." A 6.5× production gap that is the product of four decades of deliberate policy choices.
IV

A 40× Gap: The HALEU Supply Crisis

The existing SWU imbalance is a known, manageable problem with contractual workarounds and a clear remediation path. The HALEU shortage is categorically different — not a gap to be managed but a supply chain that largely does not yet exist.

Western enrichment cascades were engineered to produce low-enriched uranium at 3–5% U-235. Reconfiguring them to produce HALEU at 20% U-235 is not a simple operational adjustment. The number of cascade stages, the stage heights, the feed and withdrawal rates — all must be redesigned. The transport containers require new criticality certifications for the higher fissile density. Fabrication lines require new equipment. Regulatory frameworks don't yet exist for commercial HALEU at scale. And the only country that already operates commercial HALEU production is Russia.

V

Four Forces Converging at Once

Four distinct pressures are arriving simultaneously, and their interaction is what makes this moment structurally different from prior nuclear energy cycles.

Demand from an unexpected quarter. Between October 2024 and the time of writing, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have collectively signed nuclear power purchase agreements totaling more than 10 gigawatts of new U.S. capacity — roughly equivalent to Spain's entire installed nuclear fleet. These are not utilities hedging grid exposure. They are hyperscale cloud operators securing 24/7 baseload power for AI infrastructure that cannot tolerate intermittency. Microsoft is restarting Three Mile Island Unit 1 under a 20-year PPA. Amazon has contracted up to 5 gigawatts of X-energy SMR output and is converting Susquehanna into a nuclear-powered AI campus. Google signed the first corporate SMR purchase agreement in history with Kairos Power. Meta committed 1.1 gigawatts to Constellation and issued an open RFP for an additional 1 to 4 gigawatts.

Direct federal capital deployment. In January 2026, the DOE allocated $2.7 billion to rebuild domestic enrichment capacity: $900 million each to Centrus Energy's American Centrifuge Operating subsidiary, to General Matter at the former Paducah Gaseous Diffusion Plant in Kentucky, and to Orano Federal Services. An additional $28 million went to Global Laser Enrichment for next-generation laser separation technology.

Stack those four forces. Add parallel capital commitments in the United Kingdom (£300 million HALEU allocation), France (Project IKE, a $5 billion Orano fabrication facility in Tennessee), Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power's forward supply contracts, and visible expansion programs in Japan, South Korea, and Eastern Europe. The total Western capital expenditure required to close the enrichment deficit over the next decade sits somewhere around $200 billion — an aggregation of publicly announced programs, private capex commitments, and utility offtake contracts that, if anything, understates the eventual total.

Three Mile Island
TMI Unit 1 — which had no role in the 1979 accident — was closed in 2019 for economic reasons. Microsoft's 20-year PPA brought it back online in September 2024: the first U.S. nuclear restart in 40 years. The plant now powers Microsoft data center operations.

The 2028 Cliff
The Russian import ban has a hard administrative sunset on January 1, 2028. Unlike a tariff, it cannot be bypassed by market participants. Post-that-date, there is no regulatory pathway to import Russian enriched uranium into the U.S. regardless of price.
IEA Projection
Global data center electricity demand is projected to exceed 945 TWh by 2030 — roughly equal to Japan's entire current annual electricity consumption. Nuclear is the only technology that can supply this at baseload scale without grid-scale storage.
VI

A Cathedral of Centrifuges in Southern Ohio

Sixty miles south of Columbus, at the end of a state highway running through the Scioto River valley, sits a building roughly the size of the Pentagon. It was constructed in the early 1950s as part of the Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant — a Cold War facility built to produce weapons-grade uranium. It sat idle after the plant was decommissioned in 2013. It is no longer idle.

Inside, a row of grey metal cylinders turns continuously — each spinning a column of uranium hexafluoride gas at supersonic velocities, mounted on magnetic bearings in vacuum chambers. They make almost no sound. They do not stop. They are producing the only HALEU currently being manufactured at commercial scale in the United States.

The building is operated by Centrus Energy, NYSE: LEU — a company that emerged from the USEC bankruptcy in 2014 and spent the subsequent years largely invisible to equity markets. There is no observation deck. There are no press tours. There is not, in the conventional sense, a story being told about this place. There is just the cascade, turning.

This distinction matters to the investment thesis. The visual language of the nuclear renaissance — sleek reactor renderings beside data centers, PowerPoint slides from SMR startups, conference panels at energy summits — is focused on a future that does not yet exist. The Piketon facility exists today. It is operating today. It is the only thing on this side of the Atlantic that is actually producing the fuel the nuclear renaissance requires.

Both the rendering and the cascade matter. But investors who have priced the rendering without pricing the cascade have their framework backwards.

Piketon
The Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant, Piketon, Ohio. Built 1952–1956. 3,700 acres. Production hall: 1,600 × 1,000 feet — larger than the Pentagon's floor plate. Decommissioned 2013. Partially re-activated under DOE contract, hosting the only U.S. HALEU cascade.

VII

Three Industries, Three Decades, One Structural Pattern

The following is a structural claim, not a coincidental observation: in any capital-constrained industrial buildout, the equity premium accrues to the irreplaceable input rather than the headline application. Three distinct cycles over the past two decades demonstrate this with enough consistency to constitute a pattern.

North American shale, 2008–2014. The investment thesis that attracted capital was production — Pioneer Natural Resources, Continental Resources, Chesapeake Energy, EOG Resources. The production thesis was correct: North American output rose dramatically. The equity returns, for most producers, were not. Chesapeake, briefly the second-largest natural gas producer in the United States, filed Chapter 11 in June 2020 with $9 billion in debt. The returns accrued to the midstream operators who controlled the pipelines and gathering systems that the resource required but couldn't quickly replicate: Enterprise Products Partners, Energy Transfer, Williams, Targa Resources. These companies charged a toll on the resource without bearing the resource's commodity price risk. The resource was abundant. The infrastructure to move it was scarce and slow to build.

Semiconductor equipment, 2014–2024. The headline story was chip design — Nvidia, AMD, Intel. The structural prize went to ASML, a Dutch company that manufactures the extreme ultraviolet lithography systems without which leading-edge semiconductor fabrication is physically impossible. ASML's share price appreciated from approximately €50 in 2014 to more than €700 at its peak — not by being the most visible actor in semiconductors but by being the one actor that the rest of the industry could not work around. One supplier. No substitute. Effectively unconstrained pricing power within the confines of the market.

Investment Framework
The Choke Point Premium
In a capacity-constrained buildout, the equity premium concentrates at the irreplaceable bottleneck through which all activity must pass. The bottleneck commands a toll on everything above it in the value chain. Its multiple expands in proportion to how slowly the scarcity can be arbitraged away — and enrichment infrastructure is among the slowest-to-build industrial assets in existence.

The 1970s nuclear buildout. The reactor manufacturers — Westinghouse, General Electric, Combustion Engineering, Babcock & Wilcox — experienced a mixed decade. Companies holding capacity for specialized large forgings required in reactor pressure vessel construction — a capability concentrated globally in a small number of facilities — outperformed the manufacturers whose designs depended on those forgings.

In the current nuclear cycle, the SMR designers are the headline — Oklo, TerraPower, X-energy, Kairos Power. The irreplaceable input is enrichment capacity, specifically HALEU production infrastructure that cannot be commissioned quickly, cheaply, or without expertise that took decades to develop and was then deliberately run down. The pattern suggests where the multiple will expand.

ASML
ASML's EUV machines weigh 180 tons, require 40 shipping containers to transport, and take months to install and calibrate. The company ships roughly 50 per year. There is no second source. It earns margins that most software businesses would envy.
"The multiple expands in proportion to how slowly the scarcity can be arbitraged away — and enrichment infrastructure is among the slowest-to-build industrial assets in existence."
Chesapeake
At its 2008 peak, Chesapeake Energy had a ~$35B market cap. Chapter 11 filing: June 2020, with $9B in debt. The shale resource was real and vast. The value of that resource did not accrue to those who extracted it.

IX

Three Categories Worth Avoiding

The distinction between research and marketing is usually found in what an analysis declines to endorse. Three categories warrant explicit avoidance.

⚠ Treat With Caution — SMR Designers
Oklo's stock has declined approximately 70% from its 2025 highs — trading around $50 against a peak of ~$170 — with market cap still near $8.7 billion against zero revenue and no NRC-certified design. The second Aurora application is under review after the first was rejected in 2022. A 12 GW master power agreement with data centre operator Switch and a U.S. Air Force contract at Eielson Air Force Base are genuine signals of access and intent — not substitutes for fuel, a certified design, or cash flow. The stock's derating from peak is precisely what the original caution anticipated: narrative got priced; execution has not arrived. NuScale (NYSE: SMR) is more defensible: it holds the only NRC Standard Design Approval for an advanced reactor, revenue growing from $31M (2025) toward analyst projections of $287M (2028), and a September 2025 TVA partnership for a 6 GW SMR programme. More defensible; still requires an investor to hold a long execution chain without slippage. Some of these companies will succeed. Which ones is a separate analytical problem from the fuel cycle thesis — and a harder one.
⚠ Avoid — Pure-Play Junior Uranium Miners
A defensible position at $20/lb uranium in 2020. A crowded, sentiment-driven trade at $80/lb. The marginal cost of new uranium production sits materially below prevailing spot prices; new entrants are arriving. Prefer the portions of the cycle where capacity is genuinely constrained and slow to add — enrichment, conversion, fabrication — over the portions where the barrier is primarily capital deployment.
⚠ Avoid — Russian Supply Workarounds
The U.S. import ban is in force. The January 1, 2028 waiver cliff is statutory, not regulatory. The political trajectory is not toward relaxation. Any thesis predicated on a U.S.–Russia nuclear fuel accommodation faces modest upside if correct and asymmetric downside — including regulatory, reputational, and compliance exposure — if incorrect. This is not a tail risk worth wearing.
NRC Timelines
NRC design certification under standard review: 7–10 years. Oklo's first Aurora application was rejected February 2022. A revised application is under review. The regulatory clock restarted. Add construction timelines to that.

X

Three Ways the Thesis Fails

Scenario 1 — Western Enrichment Capacity Arrives Faster Than Expected
Centrus completes Phase III on schedule and under budget. General Matter executes cleanly at Paducah. Orano's Project IKE compresses. Urenco's New Mexico expansion finishes ahead of its 2027 target. The structural bottleneck closes by 2028–2029 and enrichment names reprice down 20–30% as the scarcity premium fades. Assessment: possible, but historically atypical. Greenfield enrichment plants have not, as a category, come in early or under budget. The DOE's own program studies show 30–50% schedule slippage as the historical norm.
Scenario 2 — SMR Deployment Slows Materially
NRC approval timelines stretch. Hyperscaler power purchase commitments soften as AI capital expenditure cycles down or power economics get re-examined. HALEU demand pushes from 2030 to 2035 or beyond. The existing-fleet LEU side of the trade still works — 94 reactors still need fuel — but HALEU optionality compresses. Cameco-Westinghouse continues compounding on the existing fleet business. The trade is still positive; the magnitude is smaller. Assessment: meaningful probability worth sizing around.
Scenario 3 — Geopolitical Shock Accelerates the Timeline
Russian export controls, a second major conflict, or sanctions tightening before the 2028 waiver cliff. The basket re-rates more violently than the base case. This is not modeled explicitly — but it is, structurally, the tail that is asymmetrically positive for the thesis, which is why conviction in this position is maintained through the noise.
Greenfield History
Urenco's New Mexico enrichment facility — the most recent large U.S. enrichment project — took 15+ years from inception to commercial operation. The DOE's historical enrichment program data shows an average schedule overrun of 30–50%.

Currently the cleanest leading indicator for the broader cycle. The enrichment supply chain has two sequential bottlenecks: conversion (uranium oxide → UF₆) and enrichment (UF₆ → enriched UF₆). When SWU prices rise faster than conversion prices, enrichment is the binding constraint. When conversion prices diverge upward, the bottleneck has shifted one step upstream. Watch for divergence between the two series.

Westinghouse IPO Chatter
The U.S. government participation structure has a five-year fuse running to January 2029. Serious IPO discussions, if they begin, will trigger sell-side modelling of Cameco's 49% stake against a $30B+ target valuation — making visible a number that is currently absent from most institutional models. Watch Cameco earnings calls for shifts in language around Westinghouse monetization.
Signal 5
Hyperscaler Fuel Supply Disclosures
None of the announced SMR power purchase agreements — Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta — has publicly disclosed a corresponding HALEU fuel supply arrangement. The first corporate to disclose one will force the rest to answer the same question in earnings calls and analyst meetings, making the thinness of the fuel pipeline legible to markets for the first time.
8-K Filings
SEC Form 8-K is filed for material events between quarterly reports — major contracts, production milestones, financing transactions. For Centrus, Phase III production milestones will be 8-K events. Setting a filing alert is the most time-efficient way to track them.
"The first hyperscaler to disclose a HALEU fuel supply plan will make the thinness of the pipeline legible to markets for the first time."
Conversion vs. SWU
When conversion prices lead SWU prices upward, the bottleneck has migrated upstream. The two-bottleneck structure of the fuel cycle means that even if enrichment capacity scales on target, a conversion shortfall can still constrain total output. Track both series independently.
XII

Canada Goes Nuclear: Ontario's C$47 Billion Commitment

While the American buildout commands most of the analytical attention, the most consequential state-level capital commitment to nuclear energy in North America may be quietly assembling itself in Ontario. In May 2025, Ontario Power Generation broke ground on Canada's first small modular reactor at the Darlington site on Lake Ontario — the same licensed land that houses four of the country's most productive CANDU units. The move was not symbolic: it was the opening position of a C$20.9 billion construction programme, with C$3 billion of anchor capital committed jointly by the federal government and the province.

The reactor design selected for Darlington is the GE-Vernova BWRX-300 — a 300 MW boiling water design that runs on conventional U-235 enrichment at standard 3–5% concentration levels, not HALEU. This distinction carries weight for the investment framework. Ontario's SMR programme will not directly create HALEU demand. What it will do is further tighten an already strained conventional SWU market — four BWRX-300 units running at capacity absorb enrichment supply that must be produced somewhere other than Russia. The pressure on the enrichment bottleneck compounds from multiple directions simultaneously.

Design Philosophy
BWRX-300: The Evolutionary Bet
Unlike the U.S. advanced SMR pipeline — which uses HALEU, novel coolants, and unproven operating geometries — the BWRX-300 is an evolutionary design: proven boiling water reactor technology scaled down, with standard U-235 fuel and a known regulatory pathway. Ontario is not betting on the physics frontier. It is betting that nuclear power at smaller scale and lower upfront capital commitment can win on economics — and that conventional fuel supply chains are adequate for that purpose. A successful Darlington build becomes the reference plant that justifies every subsequent BWRX-300 order in the Western pipeline.

In November 2025, Ontario separately approved Ontario Power Generation's plan to refurbish Pickering Units 5 through 8. At C$26.8 billion, this is among the largest single industrial procurement authorisations in Canadian history. It secures 2,200 MW of producing baseload capacity for four additional decades. Pickering's continued operation locks in a decades-long demand commitment for uranium and enrichment services that suppliers can contract against today. Reactors, once refurbished and operating, do not get switched off for price reasons.

At the supply end of the chain, Cameco has moved decisively to reposition itself as the Western world's preferred fuel services supplier beyond mining. In 2023, Cameco signed an agreement with Ukraine's Energoatom to supply 100% of Energoatom's natural uranium hexafluoride from 2024 through 2035 — rerouting all of Ukraine's nine-reactor fleet from Russian to Canadian supply in a single commercial agreement. This is supply chain decoupling implemented as a term sheet rather than a policy speech.

Darlington Site
The existing Darlington Nuclear Generating Station — four CANDU units, 3,524 MW — underwent a C$12.8B refurbishment beginning 2016. The SMR units are being built on the same licensed 1,300-acre site, reusing grid connections, trained operators, and site approvals that would otherwise take 10–15 years to obtain from scratch.
"Ontario is not betting on the physics frontier. It is betting that nuclear power at smaller scale can win on economics — and that conventional fuel supply chains are adequate."
Not Directly Investable
Ontario Power Generation is a Crown corporation — wholly owned by the Province of Ontario, not publicly listed. The investable expression of Ontario's nuclear build is indirect: Cameco (CCJ) as fuel supplier, GE Vernova (NYSE: GEV) as reactor vendor, and any publicly listed contractors winning Darlington sub-contracts.
Nova Scotia MoU
In late 2025, Nova Scotia signed an SMR memorandum of understanding with Ontario — signalling that provincial nuclear momentum is spreading beyond the initial anchor site, potentially adding further BWRX-300 units from Maritime utilities.
XIII

Two Wars and the Fuel Cycle

Two conflicts that began before this thesis was written have developed in ways that materially intersect with the nuclear fuel cycle — one by steadily enforcing supply chain bifurcation, the other by removing a geopolitical variable from the board entirely and with unusual finality.

Ukraine. In February and March 2026, Russia and Ukraine agreed to narrow local ceasefires around Zaporizhzhia — operational arrangements brokered by the IAEA to enable repair of a critical 330 kV backup power line, not a peace overture. The plant remains under Russian military control and is the central nuclear energy issue in any eventual settlement. The U.S.-backed framework for trilateral management is one that Ukraine rejects on the grounds that shared operational control legitimises the occupation it is fighting to reverse. Meanwhile, the enrichment market has bifurcated.

The Plant at the Centre
Zaporizhzhia — 5,700 MW in a Three-Way Stalemate
Europe's largest nuclear power station: six VVER-1000 reactors, 5,700 MW — roughly equal to Switzerland's entire electricity grid. All six units in cold shutdown since late 2022. The plant consumes significant power just to maintain safety systems, drawing from the same grid it once supplied. Who controls it when conflict ends is not a technical question. It is a sovereignty question with no agreed answer, and it sits at the centre of peace negotiations with no resolution in sight as of May 2026.

Russia's Rosatom reported foreign revenues declining to $16.5 billion in 2025, down from more than $18 billion the prior year — a measurable consequence of the U.S. import ban, European diversification programmes, and the reputational cost of operating nuclear assets in an active war zone. The capacity Rosatom is losing in Western markets is not idle: China significantly increased its purchases of Russian enriched uranium in 2025. The enrichment market is splitting into Western and Sino-Russian tracks with structural permanence that grows more entrenched each year the war continues.

Ukraine Peace Scenario — What Changes and What Doesn't
A negotiated settlement would not restore Rosatom access to Western utilities. The U.S. import ban is statutory, not executive — reversing it requires Congressional action, not a diplomatic agreement. European utilities that have spent two years diversifying away from Russian supply will not re-entangle voluntarily. What peace talks could do is reduce the urgency premium in Western procurement — if policymakers perceive the threat as structurally contained, the pace of enrichment buildout authorisations may slow at the margin. Net assessment: the structural thesis survives a peace deal; the urgency premium softens temporarily.

Iran. The Iran development is the most dramatic event in the nuclear space since this thesis was first articulated. In June 2025, following the collapse of U.S.–Iran nuclear talks, Israel launched Operation Rising Lion. U.S. strikes on Iranian nuclear sites followed. What became known as the Twelve-Day War (June 13–24, 2025) rendered most of Iran's known enrichment infrastructure inoperable within a fortnight. Iran formally terminated the JCPOA in October 2025.

June–October 2025
The Twelve-Day War — Damage and Aftermath
Natanz: Comprehensive destruction of enrichment halls and cascade infrastructure. No repair activity in satellite imagery through end-2025.

Fordow: IAEA Director General confirmed September 2025 that "almost all sensitive equipment" at the buried facility was destroyed.

Pentagon assessment (July 2025): Iran's nuclear programme set back approximately two years.

JCPOA: Formally terminated by Iran on October 18, 2025 — the diplomatic architecture for supervised civilian enrichment no longer exists.

Iran was not a significant participant in the commercial enrichment market — its programme was weapons-adjacent rather than oriented toward civilian fuel production. The direct SWU impact is near-zero. What the Twelve-Day War forecloses is option value: scenarios in which a future agreement brought Iranian civil enrichment capacity into the Western market, or created competitive pressure on the global SWU price. Both paths are effectively closed. The more interesting second-order effect is on Gulf state civilian nuclear programmes — and therefore on Western fuel demand.

Iran Aftermath — Indirect Positives for the Thesis
With Iranian enrichment capacity destroyed and the JCPOA terminated, Gulf state civilian nuclear programmes are likely to accelerate — both as energy development and as a strategic hedge. Saudi Arabia has issued tenders for sixteen large reactors. The UAE's Barakah plant (5,600 MW, four units, fully operational) runs on Western-supplied fuel. Neither country will source enrichment services from Russia or a post-war Iran — adding a Gulf demand vector for Western enrichment capacity that most models have not yet quantified.
Iran Tail Risk — Clandestine Reconstitution
Iran retains enriched uranium stockpiles, a technical workforce, and both the motive and prior precedent for undeclared nuclear activity. A clandestine reconstitution discovered publicly 12–24 months from now would introduce a geopolitical shock cycle into uranium markets — brief spike in nuclear positioning, followed by policy uncertainty. Not the base case. A monitoring condition worth tracking via IAEA quarterly safeguards reports and satellite imagery analysis from groups such as the Institute for Science and International Security.
Rosatom's New Geography
Rosatom is not contracting — it is redirecting. The company currently has 22 reactor units under construction abroad, almost all in non-Western jurisdictions. Russian nuclear influence is not declining globally; it is decoupling from the Western track specifically, while China absorbs the diverted enrichment capacity.
"The enrichment market is splitting into Western and Sino-Russian tracks with structural permanence that grows more entrenched each year the war continues."
JCPOA — 2015 to 2025
Signed Vienna, July 2015. Iran's enrichment capped at 3.67%; stockpile at 300 kg. Iran began withdrawing from commitments in January 2020 after U.S. maximum-pressure sanctions. The October 2025 formal termination ended what remained of the framework entirely. A decade of diplomacy, closed in a fortnight of airstrikes.
Gulf Nuclear Scale
Saudi Arabia's planned 16-reactor programme, if built, would require enrichment supply comparable to the entire current U.S. civilian fleet. The UAE's Barakah — four APR-1400 units, 5,600 MW, built by Korea Electric Power — is fully operational and runs on Western-supplied fuel. Neither programme has a confirmed long-term Western enrichment arrangement in place.
